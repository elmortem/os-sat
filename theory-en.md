# OS-SAT — Open Source Space Accretion Theory

This is not a scientific publication and not a physics paper. It is a **science-fiction theory** — a speculative idea, written up for discussion, criticism, and possible use in fiction, worldbuilding, games, and essays. The core thought is just one: **space does not simply expand — it statistically accretes new elements**. Consequences are drawn from this assumption — but it remains a mental construct, not a description of the real Universe.

## The starting idea

Suppose space is not an empty stage but a physical object with its own internal structure.

In its initial state, it is:

```text
static
homogeneous
uniform
flat
with very low entropy
with matter distributed almost uniformly
```

Then new elementary patches of space begin to appear within it.

Not "a little cube is inserted somewhere into emptiness", but rather:

```text
geometry changes locally
a new patch of distance appears
volume increases
relations between neighboring regions change
```

The process itself is statistical:

```text
on average — uniform
locally — fluctuating
each individual event is unpredictable
the overall rate of the process is stable
```

Analogy: like radioactive decay. We do not know which specific particle will decay, but we know the statistical law of the process.

---

## How inhomogeneities appear

If new patches of space appeared perfectly uniformly, the Universe would just expand.

But if the process is discrete and statistical, small fluctuations are inevitable:

```text
somewhere space accreted slightly more
somewhere slightly less
```

Where space accreted more, matter becomes more rarefied.

Where space accreted less, matter remains relatively denser.

Then ordinary gravity kicks in:

```text
denser regions attract matter more strongly
less dense regions hold it less well
voids become emptier
dense regions become denser
```

A positive feedback loop arises:

```text
small difference
→ small redistribution of matter
→ amplification of gravitational contrast
→ formation of voids, filaments, clouds, galaxies
```

In this model, inhomogeneities appear not because "space pushes matter", but because **uneven accretion of space changes density and the gravitational balance**.

---

## Why voids start growing faster

An important point: new patches of space are not required to "prefer" voids.

It is enough that they appear uniformly **across already existing space**.

When voids occupy more volume, statistically more new patches of space appear inside them:

```text
more empty volume
→ more places for new patches to appear
→ voids receive a larger share of expansion
→ empty volume grows even more
```

This is not a new law, but a consequence of geometry and statistics.

From this a mechanism for accelerated expansion can be derived:

```text
the larger the share of voids,
the more the average expansion of the Universe is determined by voids
```

That is, accelerated expansion may not be a separate "dark energy", but an effect of the growing share of empty space.

---

## Why bound systems do not expand

In the observable Universe, atoms, molecules, planets, the Solar System, and galaxies do not expand together with cosmological expansion.

This fits the model well.

New patches of space effectively appear where there is no strong local binding:

```text
intergalactic voids — expand
galaxies — barely expand
star systems — do not expand
atoms — do not expand
```

Local forces and gravitational binding hold the structure together.

So the model does not have to claim that "everything stretches equally". On the contrary, its logic is:

```text
bound matter resists expansion
voids absorb the bulk of space accretion
```

---

## Where the model is potentially better than the inflationary one

Inflation introduces a separate early mechanism of ultra-fast expansion. Usually this is associated with a special field or state that caused space to expand sharply, and then for some reason stopped.

Problem: both the start of inflation and its abrupt end themselves require explanation.

In the space-accretion model there is simply no inflation.

```text
the rate at which new patches of space appear is constant
no early ultra-fast expansion ever occurred
no separate phases exist
```

The problems for which inflation was introduced are solved here not by accelerated expansion, but through the properties of the initial state and the accretion process itself.

The horizon problem.
Distant regions of the sky have almost the same temperature, although under ordinary expansion they were never causally connected. Inflation solves this by stretching one small connected region. In this model, the problem is removed differently: the initial state of space was already homogeneous and uniform. Homogeneity is not the result of causal equalization, but an initial postulate. Constant accretion across existing space does not break it.

The flatness problem.
The almost exact flatness of the observable Universe in the standard picture requires fine-tuning or smoothing by inflation. In this model, flatness is the initial state. Uniform statistical accretion does not curve it systematically: local fluctuations exist, but on average they do not accumulate curvature.

The magnetic monopole problem.
Here the model removes the question in the same way as the previous two: if the initial state does not produce monopoles in excess, no inflation is needed to dilute them. This point is less certain — it depends on how exactly early-Universe physics is arranged in this model.

On scales much larger than the observable Universe, fluctuations may well exist. The model does not require the whole world to be homogeneous. It only says that the observable region emerged from a homogeneous initial state, and constant accretion does not destroy that state.

The strong side of the model:

```text
one mechanism explains:
- expansion
- the appearance of inhomogeneities
- the growth of voids
- possible accelerated expansion
```

And the problems for which inflation was introduced — horizon, flatness, monopoles — are removed not by a separate mechanism, but by properties of the initial state plus the accretion process itself.

This is conceptually more economical than a set of separate entities:

```text
inflaton
dark energy
primordial fluctuations
separate explanation of structure growth
separate explanation of inflation's start and end
```

## Where both models have insufficient assumptions

The inflationary model has its strengths: it explains homogeneity, near-flat geometry, and the character of primordial fluctuations well.

But it has its starting assumptions:

```text
why did inflation begin?
why was the inflaton field exactly the way it was?
why did inflation end?
what was happening beyond our observable region?
is the unobservable Universe the same?
```

The space-accretion model also has starting assumptions:

```text
why was the initial space static?
why was it flat?
why was it homogeneous and uniform?
why did the process of new patches appearing begin?
why is the rate of the process exactly what it is?
```

So the honest position is this:

> Inflation is not free of fundamental assumptions. The space-accretion model is also not free of them. The difference lies in which assumptions we consider more natural.

Inflation takes as natural:

```text
an early small region was causally connected,
then stretched sharply
```

The space-accretion model takes as natural:

```text
initial space was a uniform phase state,
and inhomogeneities appeared only after its discrete growth began
```

Both positions require an initial postulate.

---

## Flatness and uniformity

In this model, the flatness of the Universe is not required to be a result of inflation.

A different basic postulate can be adopted:

```text
zero curvature — initial state of space
```

And the uniform statistical appearance of new patches creates no systematic curvature:

```text
local fluctuations exist
but on average they average out toward the initial state
```

That is:

```text
initially flat space
+ uniform accretion
= almost flat space on large scales
```

This is not a proof but a rule of the model. But its status is no worse than the inflationary postulate of a special early phase of expansion.

---

## Where the model currently loses

The main loss right now is **dark matter**.

Accelerated expansion through void growth looks relatively natural:

```text
more voids
→ new patches more often land in voids
→ voids grow even faster
```

But dark matter is a different effect.

It is needed not only for the overall expansion picture, but to explain the behavior inside and around galaxies:

```text
stars at the outskirts of galaxies rotate too fast
galaxies behave as if surrounded by an invisible massive halo
galaxy clusters produce strong gravitational lensing
structures grew faster than ordinary matter alone would allow
```

In the standard picture, dark matter sits in large halos around galaxies.

For the space-accretion model to replace dark matter, one would have to show that space accretion produces the same gravitational profile around galaxies:

```text
not just rarefaction of voids
but an effect of additional attraction around galaxies
```

This has not been shown.

A possible working idea:

```text
at the boundary between a gravitationally bound galaxy
and the expanding external space,
a metric-gravitational gradient arises
that looks like an invisible mass halo
```

But this already requires mathematics.

Without calculations, one cannot honestly say that dark matter has been explained.

## The current formulation of the model

Provisionally, the model can be written as follows:

```text
1. Space is a discrete dynamic object.

2. The initial state of space:
   static, flat, homogeneous, uniform,
   with very low entropy and almost uniform matter.

3. New elements of space appear statistically uniformly
   across already existing space.

4. Individual events are unpredictable,
   but the average rate of the process is stable.

5. Local fluctuations in space accretion produce
   small differences in matter density.

6. Regions with greater accretion become less dense.

7. Regions with smaller accretion remain relatively denser.

8. Gravity amplifies this contrast.

9. Voids grow faster because they take up an ever-larger share of the volume.

10. Bound systems do not expand,
    because local binding is stronger than cosmological accretion.

11. Accelerated expansion may be a consequence of the growing share of voids.
```

---

## Honest status

This model is not proven, not formalized mathematically, and does not claim to be correct.

The correct status is:

> This is a conceptual framework for an alternative cosmological model in which expansion, fluctuations, and accelerated expansion are derived from a single process — the statistical birth of new patches of space.

Strengths:

```text
single mechanism
natural amplification of voids
clear statistical nature of fluctuations
possible replacement of dark energy
removal of horizon, flatness, and (probably) monopole problems without inflation
```

Weaknesses:

```text
no equations
dark matter not explained
no link to the cosmic microwave background described
no exact law of space accretion specified
no clarity on how to test the model against observations
```

Bottom line:

> The model still loses to standard cosmology in calculations, testability, and number of Nobel prizes, but wins as an intuitively coherent attempt to reduce expansion, fluctuations, and acceleration to one single process. The next serious step is not "to prove the Universe", but to write down a minimal set of rules and understand which observable effects they must reproduce.
